HAPS Communications
Payload
Sep 2025 – Apr 2026 · UBC Okanagan
LoRaWi-Fi HaLowLTERaspberry PiRF Systems
The Challenge

When conventional infrastructure fails — in remote regions or during disasters — people lose communication. Our client, Dr. Anas Chaaban at UBCO, challenged our team to build a proof-of-concept wireless relay payload suitable for a high-altitude balloon platform.

The result is a modular multi-protocol communications system integrating LoRa, Wi-Fi HaLow, and LTE on a single Raspberry Pi-based payload — 591 g, under $375 CAD, ~5 hours of battery runtime. The software runs the Reticulum network stack for routing and LXMF for messaging, enabling reliable low-bandwidth communication across multiple radio hops. I contributed to LoRa testing, enclosure development, 3D printing, and final integration.

Field testing across six locations in Kelowna, BC validated a 5.4 km LoRa link at 75% link quality, and the full end-to-end internet chain (Client → HaLow → HAP → LTE → Internet) was verified with a live speed test.

The Project

Our group designed and built a custom outrunner brushless DC motor and programmable controller from scratch — mechanically, electrically, and in firmware. The goal was to develop hands-on understanding of the full motor drive chain, from electromagnetic geometry through commutation to smooth output torque.

The motor features a 3D-printed rotor shell and stator, a three-phase wye-wound stator, and a permanent-magnet rotor. The team developed a KiCad PCB and STM32 firmware implementing sensorless field-oriented control (FOC). My personal contributions were in controller testing and design assistance — validating the drive electronics, debugging commutation behavior, and contributing to the firmware tuning that brought the prototype to a running state.

ENGR 352 — Microelectronics II
Optical PPG
Heart-Rate Sensor
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
Analog DesignLTSpiceTIABiomedical
The Challenge

PPG sensors measure heart rate by detecting microscopic variations in reflected light from pulsing blood — but the signal is tiny and rides on a large DC offset from ambient light. Extracting a reliable pulse requires a carefully designed analog chain at every stage.

I designed the complete signal path from photodiode to digital pulse output: a transimpedance amplifier (TIA) to convert photodiode current to voltage, AC-coupled amplification to block the DC offset, an active bandpass filter targeting 0.5–5 Hz, a Schmitt trigger comparator for hysteresis and edge detection, and a one-shot pulse shaper for a clean digital output.

The design used hand calculations → LTSpice simulation → breadboard verification, confirming that simulation matched real hardware behavior.

The Project

A full-custom 3-bit 3-input full adder in 130 nm CMOS, designed from gate level to LVS-verified physical layout in Cadence Virtuoso — following the same flow used in professional IC tape-outs.

The project covered gate-level and transistor-level design, transient simulation of all input combinations, delay and power extraction, and corner simulation (FF/SS process, voltage, and temperature variation). The 1-bit adder layout was completed with a clean LVS pass. A bonus 3-bit full layout was also pursued — each stage built from the verified 1-bit cell with carry routing between stages.

ENGR 473 — RF & Microwave Engineering
Beverage Antenna
Design & Simulation
Sep 2025 – Dec 2025 · UBC Okanagan
AWREM SimulationHF Antenna
The Challenge

The Beverage is a classical HF travelling-wave antenna used in amateur radio, shortwave listening, and military HF systems — valued for its directivity and low-elevation pattern. Its challenge: it is electrically enormous at HF frequencies, making standard EM simulation tools difficult or impossible to apply directly.

Starting from published papers and first-principles antenna theory, I designed a Beverage geometry and built an iterative simulation workflow in AWR Microwave Office. To work around AWR's computational limits with electrically large structures, I scaled the antenna to a higher frequency — a valid approach since the physics scale with wavelength. I then tuned the geometry across multiple simulation runs to optimize gain, directivity, and beam direction.

The most significant challenge was the termination resistor: AWR's schematic port only shows impedance/reflection, and adding a resistor directly to the EM structure port does not work. My solution was to approximate the resistor as a via to ground in the EM structure — a recognized limitation at microwave frequencies, but a practical and effective workaround for a scaled HF simulation.

AWR EM Structure & Simulation Results
Fig 1. Beverage Antenna — 3D EM Structure in AWR Microwave Office. The simulation geometry showing two key elements: the horizontal wire (grey, running along the top) representing the Beverage antenna conductor, and the vertical orange element representing the via-to-ground used to approximate the termination resistor. The surrounding box defines the simulation boundary and substrate. This structure was the basis for all EM simulations below — frequency-scaled to make the electrically large HF antenna tractable within AWR's computational limits.
Fig 2. Termination Resistor — Via-to-Ground Approximation. Real and imaginary parts of input impedance (Z11) after applying the via-to-ground workaround. AWR cannot place a lumped resistor directly inside an EM structure — this via approximation was engineered as a practical solution to introduce the termination loss required for travelling-wave operation.
Fig 3. Antenna Length Selection — Resonant Length. Input impedance vs. frequency for the chosen wire length. Rather than tuning for a flat travelling-wave impedance at this stage, the length was chosen to place a resonance at the desired center frequency. This establishes a good starting point before adding the termination resistor — a resonant length ensures that after the resistor is introduced, the minimum reflection still falls at the center frequency and at its 1.5 GHz multiples (e.g. 3 GHz center → next minimum at ~4.5 GHz), giving predictable and usable bandwidth at each harmonic.
Fig 4. Reflection Bandwidth — Minimum Return Loss Points. S11 in dB showing the frequency bands of best impedance match after geometry tuning. The marked valley frequencies identify where power transfer into the antenna is maximized — lower return loss means less reflected power.
Fig 5. Reflection Bandwidths — Full Frequency Sweep. Broadband return loss (S11) across the full simulation range. The plot identifies the usable operating bandwidths — defined as the frequency regions where S11 falls below −10 dB, the standard threshold in antenna design for acceptable impedance matching. The valleys occur at the center frequency and at multiples of 1.5 GHz from it (e.g. center at 3 GHz → next valley near 4.5 GHz), a direct consequence of the resonant length chosen in Fig 3. Each valley below −10 dB represents a usable operating band.
Fig 6. XZ Plane — E Field Radiation Pattern. Azimuthal polar cut confirming the Beverage antenna's characteristic end-fire directivity. The main lobe points along the wire axis, validating that the design achieves the intended beam direction. This directional gain is the defining property that makes the Beverage antenna effective for receiving distant HF stations from a specific geographic region.
Fig 7. YZ Plane — H Field Radiation Pattern. Elevation polar cut confirming low-angle radiation — the other critical property of the Beverage antenna. The pattern peaks at low elevation angles, enabling reception of low-elevation sky-wave and ground-wave HF signals from distant transmitters, rather than local near-vertical signals.

Flyback Converter
UBC Okanagan

The Project

A 100W isolated flyback DC-DC converter designed to deliver a regulated 20V/5A output from a wide input range of 90–225 VDC. The flyback topology was chosen for its galvanic isolation — the primary and secondary share no common reference, protecting the load from hazardous transients such as lightning-induced spikes.

The design process began with transformer turns ratio selection (1:3, secondary:primary) to keep MOSFET voltage stress manageable while maintaining reasonable duty cycles across the full input range. Magnetizing inductance was set to 250 µH to ensure continuous conduction at half-load, and output capacitance was selected to hold output ripple below 0.4 Vpp.

A closed-loop PI controller was designed for voltage regulation — the proportional path handles fast transient response and the integral path eliminates steady-state offset, keeping output at exactly 20V regardless of load or input variation. Component stress analysis drove semiconductor selection: the FDP12N35 MOSFET (350V/12A) and MBR6200F Schottky diode (200V/6A) were chosen with appropriate safety margins above worst-case calculated stresses.

The complete design was verified in PSIM simulation across four operating conditions — full and half load at both 90V and 225V input — with all simulation waveforms confirming alignment with hand calculations.
