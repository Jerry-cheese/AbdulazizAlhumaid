HAPS Communications
Payload
Sep 2025 – Apr 2026 · UBC Okanagan
LoRaWi-Fi HaLowLTERaspberry PiRF Systems
The Challenge

When conventional infrastructure fails — in remote regions or during disasters — people lose communication. Our client, Dr. Anas Chaaban at UBCO, challenged us to build a proof-of-concept wireless relay payload that could be lifted on a high-altitude balloon to restore emergency connectivity.

The result is a multi-protocol communications platform integrating LoRa, Wi-Fi HaLow, and LTE on a single modular Raspberry Pi-based payload — 591 g, under $375 CAD, with up to 5 hours of battery runtime. I contributed to LoRa module testing, enclosure development, 3D printing, and final system assembly and integration.

The system operates in the 915 MHz ISM band under ISED licence-exempt regulations. The software stack runs the Reticulum network protocol for routing and LXMF for messaging — allowing reliable low-bandwidth communication even across multiple radio hops.

ENGR 320 — Electromechanical Devices
Custom Brushless DC
Motor & Controller
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
STM32KiCadFOC3D Printing
The Project

Our group designed and built a custom outrunner brushless DC motor and programmable controller entirely from scratch — mechanically, electrically, and in firmware. The goal was to develop hands-on understanding of the full motor drive chain, from electromagnetic geometry to commutation algorithms.

The motor features a 3D-printed rotor shell and stator former, a three-phase wye-wound stator, and a permanent-magnet rotor. As a team, we also developed a KiCad PCB and STM32 firmware implementing sensorless field-oriented control (FOC), enabling smooth and controllable operation during bench testing without hall-effect sensors.

My personal contributions focused on controller testing and design assistance — validating the drive electronics, debugging commutation issues, and contributing to the firmware tuning process that brought the prototype to a working test-ready state.

ENGR 352 — Microelectronics II
Optical PPG
Heart-Rate Sensor
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
Analog DesignLTSpiceTIABiomedical
The Challenge

Photoplethysmography (PPG) measures heart rate by detecting microscopic variations in reflected light caused by pulsing blood volume. The signal is tiny — riding on a large DC offset from ambient light — and must be extracted reliably by a circuit designed with care at every stage.

I designed the complete analog signal chain from photodiode to digital pulse output. The chain begins with a transimpedance amplifier (TIA) to convert photodiode current into voltage, followed by AC-coupled amplification to lift the AC cardiac signal above the DC bias, an active bandpass filter targeting 0.5–5 Hz, a Schmitt trigger comparator for hysteresis and clean edge detection, and a one-shot pulse shaper for a uniform digital output.

The entire design was developed using hand calculations, then verified in LTSpice simulation, then confirmed to match on breadboard — a clean simulation-to-hardware workflow that gave me strong confidence in the final result.

The Project

VLSI design bridges circuit theory and physical silicon. In this project, I designed a 3-bit 3-input full adder in 130 nm CMOS — from transistor-level schematic through physical layout to verified silicon, following the same design flow used in professional IC development.

The design process covered gate-level logic, transistor-level implementation, transient simulation across all input combinations, delay extraction, power analysis, and corner simulation (FF and SS process corners with temperature and voltage variation) — the full set of checks required before a real tape-out.

The 1-bit full adder layout was then completed in Cadence Virtuoso with a passing LVS (Layout versus Schematic) verification — confirming that every physical transistor connection matches the schematic netlist. A bonus layout of the full 3-bit adder was also pursued for extra marks.

ENGR 473 — RF & Microwave Engineering
Beverage Antenna
Design & Simulation
Sep 2025 – Dec 2025 · UBC Okanagan
Antenna DesignAWREM SimulationHF
The Challenge

The Beverage antenna is a classical HF receiving antenna used by serious amateur radio operators, shortwave listeners, and military HF systems. It achieves remarkable directivity and low-elevation gain by exploiting a long wire above lossy ground — but its electrically large geometry creates a fundamental problem: standard EM simulation tools were not designed to handle it.

Starting from published literature and first-principles antenna theory, I designed a Beverage antenna geometry and built an iterative simulation workflow in AWR Microwave Office. I tuned the geometry and parameters across multiple simulation cycles to optimize gain, directivity, and beam direction toward the desired azimuth.

The most significant engineering challenge was the termination resistor — critical to the antenna's directional behavior — which AWR could not model directly. Adding the resistor via a schematic port only showed impedance and reflection, while connecting it to the EM structure's port did not work as expected. My solution was to approximate the resistor as a via to ground, which — while not a perfect lumped-element model at microwave frequencies — provided a valid and practical workaround within the tool's constraints for HF-scaled simulations.

1

Initial Design
Translated Beverage antenna theory from published papers into a workable baseline geometry. Defined the wire length, height above ground, and characteristic impedance targets based on the desired operating frequency and end-fire beam direction.
2
Simulation Loop
Iterative EM Tuning
Built and iterated the EM simulation workflow in AWR, scaling the antenna to a higher frequency to work within AWR's computational constraints (full-scale HF antennas are too electrically large). Tuned geometry parameters across multiple runs to reach target gain and directivity.
3
Engineering Workaround
Resistor Approximation
Solved AWR's inability to directly model the termination resistor by approximating it as a via to ground in the EM structure. Recognized and documented the limitations of this approach at high frequency, while validating it as a practical and effective solution for the scaled simulation.
Applications of the Beverage Antenna
Amateur Radio / DX
Favoured by HF contest and DX stations as a receive-only antenna on noisy bands (160m, 80m) due to excellent directivity and low noise pickup. Ideal for pulling in distant foreign stations at night.
Commercial & Military HF
Deployed at HF communication sites as receive antennas where reliability of long-range links is critical — including intelligence/surveillance applications requiring improved SNR on specific HF channels.
Ionospheric Research
Used in ionospheric sounding and propagation experiments to study low-angle sky-wave arrivals and ground-wave components, enabled by the antenna's inherently low elevation pattern.
Direction-Selective Arrays
Multiple Beverages combined into switchable or phased arrays to cover several azimuth directions — letting an operator quickly select the best receiving direction as propagation conditions change.
