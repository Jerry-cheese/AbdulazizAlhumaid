HAPS Communications
Payload
Sep 2024 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
LoRaWi-Fi HaLow LTERaspberry Pi RF Systems
The Challenge

When conventional infrastructure fails — in remote regions or during disasters — people lose communication. Our client, Dr. Anas Chaaban at UBCO, posed the question: could a small, affordable payload lifted on a high-altitude balloon provide emergency wireless connectivity?

The answer we delivered was a proof-of-concept communications relay integrating three independent RF technologies on a single modular platform weighing just 591 g and costing under $375 CAD.

I contributed to LoRa module testing, 3D-printed enclosure development, prototype assembly, and the final system integration. The project required balancing range, power budget, mass, and regulatory compliance under ISED licence-exempt spectrum rules — all simultaneously.

Design Evolution — Three Iterations
1
MVP Baseline
LoRa-Only Core
Established the foundational long-range link using LoRa 915 MHz on a Raspberry Pi. Validated bidirectional text messaging. Low power, low cost — but bandwidth-limited to ~50 kbps. Became the non-negotiable backbone for all further iterations.
2
Iteration 2
+ Wi-Fi HaLow
Added Sub-1GHz Wi-Fi HaLow (Morse Micro MM6108) to provide a higher-bandwidth local link. Required custom kernel driver compilation and systemd service integration. Achieved ~4.9 Mbps throughput at −61 dBm RSSI indoors, exceeding the 2 kbps requirement by 2,000×.
3
Final Design
+ LTE Backhaul
Integrated a SIM7670G LTE modem for internet backhaul, enabling the full communication chain: Client → HaLow → HAP → LTE → Internet. Achieved 0.39 Mbps download / 0.45 Mbps upload via Rogers Wireless — sufficient for the emergency text-messaging use case.

ENGR 467 — Electromechanical Systems
Custom Brushless DC
Motor & Controller
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
STM32KiCad FOCFirmware3D Printing
The Challenge

Most motor control courses use off-the-shelf motors. We built ours from scratch — mechanically, electrically, and in firmware. The goal was to deeply understand every stage of the drive chain, from the electromagnetic geometry of the rotor all the way to the commutation algorithm.

The motor is a custom outrunner brushless DC design with a 3D-printed rotor shell and stator frame, a three-phase wye-wound stator, and a permanent-magnet rotor. I took direct ownership of the coil winding process and helped troubleshoot the air-gap closure that was preventing the prototype from spinning.

For the controller, I developed a KiCad PCB and STM32 firmware implementing sensorless field-oriented control (FOC). FOC was chosen over simple six-step commutation because it produces smoother torque, lower electrical noise, and more reliable start-up — particularly important without hall-effect sensors to provide rotor position feedback.

Build Process
1
Mechanical
Motor Build
3D-printed outrunner shell and stator former. Wye-wound the three-phase stator by hand — a meticulous process where winding symmetry directly determines motor balance and back-EMF quality. Iterated on core alignment and air-gap closure to reach a test-ready prototype.
2
Electrical
PCB Design
Designed the three-phase inverter PCB in KiCad — gate drivers, bootstrap circuits, bulk capacitance, and MCU interface headers. Careful attention to high-current switching loop minimization and thermal layout to keep MOSFET dissipation manageable during bench testing.
3
Firmware
Sensorless FOC
Implemented sensorless FOC in C on STM32. The algorithm estimates rotor position from back-EMF zero-crossings, then applies space-vector PWM to the three phases. This enabled smooth, controllable operation during bench testing without additional hall-effect sensors or encoders.
ENGR 467 — Analog Systems
Optical PPG
Heart-Rate Sensor
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
Analog DesignLTSpice TIAComparatorBiomedical
The Challenge

Photoplethysmography (PPG) is how your smartwatch measures heart rate — it shines light into skin and measures the variation in reflected light caused by pulsing blood volume. The signal is tiny, noisy, and riding on a large DC offset from ambient light. Building a circuit that reliably extracts a clean pulse waveform requires careful analog design at every stage.

I designed the complete signal chain from photodiode to digital pulse output. Starting with a transimpedance amplifier (TIA) to convert the photodiode's current into a measurable voltage, I then added AC-coupled amplification to remove the DC offset and increase gain on the small AC signal, an active bandpass filter to reject noise outside the cardiac frequency band (0.5–5 Hz), a Schmitt trigger comparator for clean edge detection, and a one-shot pulse shaper for a uniform digital output pulse.

The design methodology went from hand calculations → LTSpice simulation → breadboard verification, confirming that simulation predictions matched real hardware behavior.

ENGR 466 — VLSI Design
Custom CMOS IC —
Logic Cells & Comparator
Jan 2025 – Apr 2025 · UBC Okanagan
CMOSCadence LVSFull-Custom Layout
The Challenge

VLSI design closes the loop between circuit theory and physical silicon. In this project, I designed and laid out a set of fundamental CMOS logic cells from transistor-level schematics all the way to verified physical layout — the same flow used in professional IC design.

The cell library included an inverter, 2-input AND gate, 2-input OR gate, and 2-input XOR gate. These were then composed into a 3-bit magnitude comparator — a practically useful circuit that determines whether two binary numbers are equal, greater, or less than each other.

Every cell went through full Layout versus Schematic (LVS) verification in Cadence to confirm that the physical transistor connections in the layout exactly match the intended schematic — a mandatory step before any real silicon tape-out.

The project built deep intuition for CMOS sizing rules, routing constraints, DRC violations, and the trade-offs between area minimization and timing closure.
